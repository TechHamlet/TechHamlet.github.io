PayPal Scam Emails Spreading Faster as Christmas Nears

Dec 18th, 2010 | Comments

PayPal Scams

The holiday season is also shopping season. To avoid long queues during last-minute shopping sprees, many people buy the items on their gift lists as early as possible. Or, if you’re among those who seriously disdain commuting or driving to the store, you’d probably go shopping online.

The holiday season is also one of the peak seasons during the year for online fraud and scams. Fraudsters rely on online shoppers’ gullibility in order to succeed. This year, scammers came as early as online shoppers in sending out their phishing emails. One of their main targets are PayPal users, since PayPal is very popular among online shoppers.

Detected as early as November this year, the latest email scam targeting PayPal users deviates from the usual phishing emails that appeared in the past. Previously, the fraudulent email only included a link to a spoof site in the message body. This time around, the message includes an attached HTML file.

Legit-Looking Sender

As with typical phishing emails, the currently circulating scam messages involving PayPal accounts are very easy to recognize. At first glance, the sender’s email address may not be a completely reliable indicator of fraud since the sender’s email address can be easily altered to make it appear as if the email came from official and genuine sources. Thus, expect official-sounding email addresses such as support@paypal.com or info@paypal.com.

The subject line of the email usually creates a sense of urgency, often prompting the recipient to act on the matter right away. The sample message that I was able to examine had the subject line “Notification of Limited Account Access” followed by some random string of letters and numbers resembling a reference code.

Non-Customized Greeting

The message body is where the fraudster will try to lure you into believing that the concern is legitimate and that you should act as advised in the email. However, simply by being alert to the way the email addresses you, you can easily spot another potential indicator of a phishing message. PayPal never greets you with something as generic as “Dear PayPal Member” or “Dear User.” Genuine emails from PayPal will always greet you with your first and last names.

Typical phishing emails contain messages that encourage you to click on the supplied link or to open an attached file. In the case of the recent PayPal-related phishing emails, the body of the message—using deceptively cordial style—tries to alert you to a security issue involving your PayPal account. The email says that because of the security issue, PayPal has restricted your account as a precautionary measure and that you need to supply “further” information which will be reviewed, and if found sufficient, PayPal will reinstate full access to your account. Then, the email instructs you to download and open the attached HTML file.

The HTML file opens a profile update form, which includes fields requesting information about your credit or debit card and home address. Amazingly, the page does look very similar to one that you might find on the official PayPal site. Yet, after you fill out the form and press the send button, the information is sent immediately—but never to PayPal.

Against Existing PayPal Policy

Just by reading the body of the email, you can determine right away that it is indeed a phishing email. PayPal has been very emphatic about its policy never to use email to collect sensitive information from you such as your credit or debit card info, bank account info, driver’s license info, email addresses, passwords, or even your real and complete name. PayPal has also been very clear in announcing that genuine emails coming from the company will never include attachments such as HTML files or software. Sadly, not all PayPal users are aware of these policies.

If you receive such an email, you should be vigilant and never act on it unless you can ascertain that it is legitimate. If you are uncertain about the veracity of the email, you can forward the entire email to spoof@paypal.com and have it verified.

Christmas and New Year are fast approaching. You can expect phishing emails to make the rounds faster, too, as the holidays approach and credit card spending also escalates. But, with your vigilance and your ability to detect phishing, you can be sure that you are not one person who will make a fraudster’s holiday wish come true.
