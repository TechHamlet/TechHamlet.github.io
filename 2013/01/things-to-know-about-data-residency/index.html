Things to Know About Data Residency

Jan 9th, 2013 | Comments

While the bird was the word in the 1960s, it seems the word currently on everyone’s lips is cloud computing. The cloud is sweeping the world by storm (pun completely intended), and is only set to grow in the future as companies and organisations become more and more mobile.

One of the most integral aspects of cloud computing (and one that many companies may not know about) is the notion of data residency. Your data and information is a vital component of your business – no matter if you’re hosting with a company like Macquarie Telecom Limited or another cloud provider, it’s important to learn more about this term and what it means.

First and foremost…data residency?

The term can seem complex, but it’s really not. Data residency literally describes where your data lives. Much like citizens have residency in certain countries, data also has residency within the country it is being hosted in. Don’t assume your data is being held in the same country your company is in – your service provider have headquarters in Japan, but their physical servers may be in the US or UK. If in doubt, always clarify this with your provider before you sign anything.

Your data is subject to the laws of its country of residence

Different countries have different laws for their citizens, and different countries also have different laws for their data ‘citizens’. If your data is stored in Australia, it is subject to Australian data laws – even if your company is based in a different country. One common piece of information you should keep in mind pertains to data laws in the US – there, all data is under regulation by the Patriot Act, which means that all data held by US companies and their subsidiaries is counted under US territorial bounds, even if they are hosted internationally.

Encrypt your data for added security

Security should always be a consideration at the forefront of any data hosting your company plans to do. When it comes to protecting your company’s data and your clients’ data, no level of security is too high. While your data centre should generally have high levels of security as it is, you can also encrypt your data for extra peace of mind.

Don’t be naïve – data residency can affect your business

Some people think that data laws will never affect them, but this isn’t always the case – just ask a New Zealand service provider who resold US-based cloud services. His company breached US copyright law, so his data and servers were seized under the Patriot Act – undoubtedly, this affected the service he provided to his clients, and his company’s reputation. Data residency is important. Don’t overlook it when you’re making your hosting decisions, and your company will be better off for it.
