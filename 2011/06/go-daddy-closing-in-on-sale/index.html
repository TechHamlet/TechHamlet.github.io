Go Daddy Closing in on Sale

Jun 28th, 2011 | Comments

Go Daddy Group Inc. may be best known as the company responsible for some risqué Super Bowl ads over the last few years (generally featuring buxom brunettes wearing skimpy clothing adorned with their logo), but they may soon be known as a different company entirely.  Since September of 2001, the site has been on the prowl for a buyer looking to spend upwards of a billion dollars for the chance to acquire their, ahem, assets.  No word yet if their famous “Go Daddy girls” are part of the deal, but anyone who purchases the company will certainly gain control of the world’s largest online domain registrar service, which is nothing to sniff at.  And now it looks like Go Daddy is close to putting the ink on the page and selling out.

It seemed like they were ready to sell as early as last September, when they were apparently considering a $1 billion deal with several private-equity firms (including General Atlantic and TPG Capital).  But the deal fell through, reportedly due to reticence on the part of Go Daddy themselves, who told buyers the auction was closed.  And it’s a good thing they held out for a better offer, because their current buyers are said to be paying between $2 billion and $2.5 billion in order to get their hands on Go Daddy, which has grown hugely despite facing some controversy from both their advertising choices and the antics of CEO Bob Parsons (who came under fire when photos surfaced of him shooting an African elephant).

In any case, buyers Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., Silver Lake Partners, and Technology Crossover Ventures seem unconcerned by any of these issues.  They are even interested enough to pay more that Go Daddy’s supposed asking price.  It was rumored that the company was looking to pull in $1.5 - $2 billion on the deal and that they had even hired Quatalyst Partners (based in San Francisco) to help them find buyers.  It’s no surprise that they needed some help, considering their strange financial position.  Although the company claims it has never posted a profit, they are currently close to $1 billion in revenue, which isn’t that remarkable to anyone who has seen their numbers.  With over 48 million domain names under their belt, as well as virtual one-stop shopping for anyone looking to make their presence known on the web, Go Daddy has made an empire out of selling and managing domain services (thanks in large part to brand recognition).

Of course, they have never gone public, which makes the sale an even sweeter deal for any potential buyer.  They filed with the SEC back in 2006, expecting to make an IPO, but failed to follow through when they realized that technology stocks were ailing on the market.  And because they are not owned by stockholders, the brokering of a sale is almost certainly easier.  As to how the transition will work once the sale is announced next week, it has been reported that Bob Parsons is expected to stay on with the company, although it is as yet unknown in what capacity.  Other than that, the new owners have remained as silent on their plans for the company as they’ve been about the purchase.
