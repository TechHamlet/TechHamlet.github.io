The Return of the Winklevoss Twins

Jun 25th, 2011 | Comments

The name may sound familiar to anyone who saw The Social Network a few months ago.  These are the good-looking phenoms who claimed to have invented Facebook only to be ripped off by technological whiz-kid Mark Zuckerberg.  Cameron and Tyler attended Harvard at the same time as Zuckerberg and during their tenure at the university, created a website very similar to Facebook known as Harvard Connect (the name was later changed to ConnectU) that was meant to be used by college students looking to connect with others on campus.  Through a series of lawsuits aimed at gaining a piece of the multi-billion dollar Facebook pie, the twins allege that Zuckerberg stole their idea and profited from it, for which they would like to be compensated.

In fact, they had settled their suit (along with a Facebook countersuit) back in 2008, with compensation valued at $65 million.  But apparently that wasn’t enough for these handsome rowers.  They filed suit again, claiming that Facebook had devalued the stock granted in the settlement to the tune of several million dollars, which made the settlement worth far less than what was granted.  Unfortunately, the case lost in appellate court, and so their only option at that point was to appeal to the Supreme Court. It seemed that all hope was lost just a few days ago when they decided not to take their suit to the next level, fearing that the outcome would not be favorable (if the Supreme Court would even hear their case).  But it appears that these swarthy youngsters won’t go down without a major fight.

Close on the heels of their loss, the Winklevoss twins have filed yet another suit in Boston alleging that Facebook suppressed information throughout the course of the original suit, in particular, instant messages penned by Zuckerberg that outline his intention to postpone his work on Harvard Connect (which he was hired to work on) in order to get Facebook up and running first.  Some of these messages were apparently taken off Zuckerberg’s own computer and leaked to the media in 2010, providing pretty clear evidence of claims made by the Winklevoss twins to the effect that Zuckerberg stole their idea (if the messages are to be believed).  Supposedly, these same messages were reviewed by Facebook lawyers and executives in 2006, before a settlement had been reached, and deemed to have no merit.  Of course, they were never turned over to opposing council.

The Winklevoss twins now claim that this is clear suppression of evidence and they have stated that they never would have agreed to a settlement had they known about the existence of these “damning” messages.  Clearly, if they had gained access to these messages sooner, they could have used them to prove their case against Zuckerberg, since the messages that were released have him explicitly outlining his plan to delay and subvert the Harvard Connect website.  And now that the Winklevoss twins have the apparent smoking gun that was missing in their case in 2008, they have decided to revisit the issue.  As to whether or not they’ll be heard, much less win, we’ll just have to watch for Social Network 2: Return of the Winklevosses.
