Firefox Can Now Stop Sites From Tracking You

May 26th, 2011 | Comments

Both desktop and mobile versions of Firefox, the world’s favorite browser, are now able to prevent sites you visit from tracking your web-browsing actions.

Starting with Firefox 4 for the desktop and the updated beta version of Firefox for Android, the browser now includes a preference setting for activating the Do Not Track (DNT) feature.

What DNT Is and Why It Is Important

Do Not Track is a feature for greater browsing privacy. Whenever you visit a website, the website and your browser actually communicate with each other—although some of the messages they send to each other are not displayed on the browser window.

Several websites monitor your browsing activities, and the process is called “tracking.” Websites do this primarily to deliver advertising that suits your preferences, as indicated by your browsing behavior. They can also use the information to provide you with customized and more relevant content.

For instance, a website can identify your regional location, and, as a result, will display the correct time, date, weather, and temperature for your region. That’s a very convenient thing, right? If your browser does not allow you to be tracked, you will need to provide the site with details (e.g., your country or ZIP code) just to receive time, date, weather, and temperature information.

How DNT Works

Many people, however, feel uncomfortable about browser tracking. Many of them feel that enabling browser tracking is just like having someone breathing down their necks. These people are often the type of people who prefer to have total control over their browsing, including control over who gets to monitor their browsing behaviors.

Firefox’s Do Not Track feature now allows such people to breathe and browse more comfortably.

DNT works by sending an HTTP header signal to the site you are browsing. If the DNT feature on your Firefox browser is enabled, Firefox will say, “DNT: 1”, that’s browser-speak for “Hey, website, do not track this user!” Of course, all these happen underneath. So, when a site that uses behavioral advertising to deliver targeted ads to you receive a “DNT: 1” message, it will stop tracking your browsing activity and will just show you generic ads instead of behavioral ads.

How to Enable DNT on an Android Device or Desktop

To enjoy the Do Not Track option on your Android phone or tablet, you need to install the latest updated beta version of Firefox for Android.

Once you already have the updated browser, you can turn on the DNT feature from the Preferences screen.

Firefox for Android is the very first mobile web browser to implement the DNT privacy feature.

For the desktop version of Firefox 4, you can turn on DNT by going to Tools > Options. On the General tab, look for the Browsing section and make sure that the box for “Tell websites I do not want to be tracked” is checked.

What about for the earlier Firefoxes? DNT was not built into the earlier versions of Firefox, but there are Firefox add-ons that you can use. The simplest is Universal Behavioral Advertising Opt-Out (UBAO). Its only job is to allow Firefox to send out the Do Not Track HTTP header. Other add-ons that also allow Firefox to enable DNT and include extra features are Adblock Plus, Do Not Track Plus, and NoScript.

Checking Your DNT Status

Want to see DNT in action? You can actually check the status of your browser’s DNT option by visiting http://dnt.mozilla.org. The DNT status indicator on the upper right corner of the right sidebar will tell you the status of your DNT option.
