Will Android Tablets Follow the Same Path as Android Phones?

Nov 11th, 2011 | Comments

Think back to three years ago. It was 2008, and Android has just made its debut. It was supposed to provide an alternative to the iPhone, which at the time thoroughly dominated the smartphone market. Because Google backed Android it made an immediate impression, but through its first year Android hardly made a dent in the smartphone market. The iPhone merely continued its dominance, still awaiting a serious competitor.

Then came November 2009 and the release of the Motorola Droid. Verizon Wireless invested heavily into marketing the device, and it paid off. Android finally had a flagship phone. A month later Google, with the help of HTC, released the Nexus One, which matched the iPhone 3GS in every technical manner. From there Android continued to roll, with more manufacturers creating handsets of all qualities. There was something for the tech geeks and there was something for the budget-conscious. All it took was a year, and Android smartphones had made a mark on the market.

While smartphones still remain an area of intense competition — both Android and iPhone seem to one-up each other constantly — the mobile battleground has recently taken on a different, larger form. Tablets have taken over as the hot gadget of 2011, and they should remain big sellers for the foreseeable future. As with smartphones, Apple struck first and they’ve managed to dominate the market. But Android has fired back. Last year they made their first major tablet release, and they’ve continually added tablets to the market. At this point they have some pretty serious iPad competition. Does this mean that Android tablets are bound to follow Android smartphones in their eventual overtaking of Apple?

One important distinction here is what we define as an Android tablet. Does that cover any tablet running the Android platform, in any form, or does it cover tablets that are powered by the Honeycomb operating system? If we count sales of the Barnes & Noble NOOK Tablet and the Amazon Kindle Fire, there’s a good chance that Android tablet sales will gain rapidly in the next few months and capture a sizable portion of Apple’s tablet market share. They might not be as powerful as high-end Android tablets, but they’re multimedia devices priced much more attractively than those high-end Android tablets. They’re also well-branded, which further helps the case.

If we’re not counting those two, it might take quite a bit longer for Android tablet sales to gain a significant share of the tablet market. One reason involves the two tablets mentioned above. Both are primed to sell big, and if they’re not considered Android tablets — and they really aren’t, since Amazon and Barnes & Noble control much of the experience — then their numbers will only hinder Android tablet development. The other issue involves price. While there are a number of quality Android tablets that are priced below the iPad, the most touted tablets are priced right in line. It’s tough to compete with Apple at that level.

Take the Samsung Galaxy Tab 10.1 for example. T-Mobile recently released the tablet with its mobile broadband service. If you follow the link you’ll see quite an attractive price point: $399.99. But that doesn’t cover half of the actual cost. Towards the bottom of the page the terms are spelled out: the full price is $650, with the remainder paid off at $10 per month. There is also a required two-year contract, and T-Mobile’s cheapest mobile broadband plan costs $40 per month. Chances are heavy data users will need a $50 or $80 monthly plan, further increasing the cost.

Even the WiFi-only Galaxy Tab checks in at the same price as the iPad. This doesn’t mean that it’s a bad tablet. In fact, it stacks up to the iPad in almost every way. The problem is in the marketing. Apple has a product that markets itself. People buy it because it’s an Apple product. Even if the Galaxy Tab is comparable or even better in every way, it still can’t match the marketing. This was a lesson learned in late 2009 and early 2010 with Android handsets. Phones such as the Nexus One and the Droid Incredible stacked up to the iPhone 3GS in terms of capabilities. But they didn’t have that marketing, and so continued to lag behind. It wasn’t until a slew of quality, affordable Android handsets hit the market that Android’s smartphone market share overtook Apple.

The good news is that the parallels are still there. In 2009 Android found its banner smartphone with the Motorola Droid. In 2011 they’ve found their banner tablet with the Samsung Galaxy Tab. As we head into 2012 we should see another crop of tablets hit the market, many of them from name-brand manufacturers at lower price points. That will help expand the Android tablet market into a sector where Apple refuses to compete. And so Android tablets should see their market share grow. Their reputation will grow with their high-end tablets, which should help fuel sales of mid-range ones.

For a while it seemed that the iPad’s lead was insurmountable. Before even one decent competitor hit the market Apple had released two smash hit tablets in the iPad and iPad 2. They’ll surely make another splash with the iPad 3 in 2012. But as was the case in 2009, Android isn’t that far behind. It might seem like a long shot now, but there’s a good chance we’ll see Android’s tablet market share slowly creep towards Apple’s, with a possible overtaking a year from now.
