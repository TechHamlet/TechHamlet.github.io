Facebook Leads to Widespread Panic!

Jul 9th, 2011 | Comments

If you are a fan of the band Widespread Panic, then you may already be aware that Facebook recently set a new precedent by selling tickets for the online viewing of the band’s two-day concert in June.  Although YouTube, Google, and others have previously streamed live performances for free, none have yet been brave enough to bank on the willingness of consumers to purchase tickets for live online viewing of bands.  But thanks to third-party platform provider Milyoni (along with production company ACL Live), this method of selling tickets to live events (albeit viewed in the privacy of one’s own home) may just be the wave of the future for concert-goers across the globe.

Milyoni is the provider of Conversational Commerce, a Facebook compatible suite of programs that allows users to monetize events through their Facebook pages.  And with the recent addition of the Social Theater platform, these services have expanded to include live streaming concerts.  This first foray into the online concert venue may seem somewhat inconsequential, with only 2,300 “ticket” sales at about $5.00 US each (50 Facebook credits), but that’s only if you’re not looking at the big picture.  So what has Facebook really accomplished, other than showing up Google and other competitors?

For starters, they have shown that people are willing to pay for online viewing of live streaming events.  So there’s that.  And although the sales were relatively negligible when compared to the sale of tickets to attend the actual event (live and in person), what is significant is that they sales occurred in 19 countries around the world (including countries in Asia, Africa, South America, and more, in addition to the US and Europe), proving a global interest in such a service (is anyone else seeing dollar signs?).  Further, it seems that this sale was really just to test the waters, since tickets were only sold via select Facebook pages (namely the sites for Widespread Panic and Austin City Limits).

Of more interest is the fact that the sites selling these virtual tickets experienced a major bump in membership following ticket sales (Austin City Limits reported a 40% increase in followers while the ranks of the Widespread Panic Facebook fan base swelled by an incredible 20,000 members).  This shows not only that consumers are interested in live streaming content, but that there is money to be made from the venture, both through ticket sales and a possible boost in membership (leading to a potential growth in advertising revenue for adjacent sites that promote these sales).  Imagine if customers who can’t see their favorite bands live suddenly start swarming Facebook fan pages for Incubus, Foo Fighters, or Adele tickets?  Everyone stands to make a lot of money.

What you may be asking yourself is just exactly how Facebook figures into this equation.  The genius part is that they really don’t.  They are simply allowing Milyoni to connect with Facebook members in order to strike a deal, so to speak.  But for this privilege they’re certainly going to take their cut.  The catch here is that these tickets, because they’re available through the Facebook portal, must be purchased via Facebook credits.  And guess what?  That means Facebook gets 30% of every sale.  It might not be much now, but it will certainly add up over time as members and consumers become aware of these events and the number of concerts offered increases.  So if you didn’t see the future in it before, perhaps you’re beginning to realize the potential scope of this service for Facebook, the music industry, and the fans.
