Easter Egg Hunt, the Tech Hamlet Way

Mar 29th, 2011 | Comments

Easter Sunday is just around the corner. With less than a month before Easter, people are already anticipating the fun and laughter of traditional Easter practices such as giving Easter gifts, Easter bunnies, and the Easter egg hunt that both young and old like.

Techie folks and geeks are fond of Easter egg hunting, too. As a matter of fact, they take the egg hunt to a different level by hunting for “Easter eggs” in digital stuff such as software, firmware, websites, electronic media (e.g., DVD movies and audio), and even computer chips and circuit boards. These virtual Easter eggs are not literal eggs. Rather, they are hidden messages, functions, or unusual behavior triggered by undocumented commands. Programmers and engineers often hide these Easter eggs in their products either for geeky humor or to display program credits.

Easter Eggs in Mozilla Firefox

If it’s your first time to hear of such Easter eggs, you might want to try some of those hidden in Mozilla Firefox. Unfortunately, I have not tested these on the latest release (Mozilla Firefox 4).

Here are some of the Easter eggs found to work in previous versions of Firefox:

  • Type about:robots in Firefox’s address bar. Press Enter. The robots have come. The first item on the list is actually the first of the three Laws of Robotics, introduced by sci-fi writer Isaac Asimov in his 1942 short story entitled “Runaround.” And, if you’re wondering about the text in the browser’s title bar, it’s the most famous command from The Day the Earth Stood Still, a sci-fi flick released in 1951.

Type about:mozilla in Firefox’s address bar and press Enter. You will see a passage from the so-called Book of Mozilla. Rumor has it that there are three versions of the passages. What appears on your browser will depend on the browser’s version.

Ever tried looking into an Infinity Mirror? It’s that mirror that reflects itself an infinite number of times. You can achieve a similar effect with Firefox. In this case, you can have an infinite number of browsers within a browser. Type chrome://browser/content/browser.xul in the Firefox address bar and hit Enter. You will find a browser window within your browser. Type the same address into the inner window to get a new inner window inside the previous one. You can do this ad infinitum—or until you run out of space or memory. Cool, huh?

Want to know who contributed to the development of Firefox? One way to find out is to click Help > About Mozilla Firefox and then press the Credits button in the dialog box. You will get a scrolling list of all the contributors. Want to get the complete, static, and non-scrolling list the geeky way? Simply type about:credits in the Firefox address bar and hit Enter.

Easter Eggs in Other Programs

There are Easter eggs in other software programs, too. For instance, these two eggs hidden in μTorrent (up to version 2.2, build 23703):

  • First, turn up your speakers’ volume. Then, click Help > About μTorrent and click on the μTorrent icon in the dialog box. I won’t spoil the surprise by telling you what will happen, but don’t worry—no loud nuclear explosion will occur.

  • Getting tired of waiting for your torrent to finish downloading/uploading? Play a classic game within μTorrent itself. Click Help > About μTorrent, then press CTRL+T. Have fun with the hidden game.

One last Easter egg for the road. This time, for the scripting language PHP (version 4 only; doesn’t work on version 5 upwards). This one’s a geeky way to find out if a website or server is running PHP4. Go to any website that serves its pages with PHP scripts (i.e., with pages having the .php extension. On the address bar, append the following string after the .php:

?=PHPE9568F36-D428-11d2-A769-00AA001ACF42

For example, http://www.copyscape.com/faqs.php?=PHPE9568F36-D428-11d2-A769-00AA001ACF42. Try it on any other site that uses PHP to serve its pages. But, take note that it will work only on servers running PHP4. Have no fear of the creature—it doesn’t bite.

There are many of such Easter eggs lying around hidden in websites and computer programs—yup, even in OpenOffice. If you are patient enough or if you are just plain lucky (say, you accidentally hit a key combination or click the mouse at the right place), you might find some of them. Go get your Easter baskets and hunt for those colorful eggs. You might want to ask Google for clues. (Share your eggs in the comments section, okay?)
