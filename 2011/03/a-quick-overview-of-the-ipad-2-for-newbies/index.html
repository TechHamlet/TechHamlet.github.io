A Quick Overview of the iPad 2 for Newbies

Mar 12th, 2011 | Comments

Many people may view the iPad as just another expensive gadget from Apple. Those are the ones who have a normal interest in technologically advanced gadgets but cannot be called “fans” of any one product, nor do they visit the manufacturer’s website on a regular basis. These people I call normal!

If someone has been influenced to visit Apple’s website and to purchase one of its products, however, it is probably because of the bullish marketing and media buzz surrounding it. Yet, non-techies are unclear on the iPad’s pros and cons. They already know what an iPad is, but they want to know exactly what it can do.

In its most basic terms, the iPad is a mobile computer. It is neither a laptop nor a personal digital assistant. It is not the first tablet computer by any means, and yet it is revolutionary.

The iPad’s width, both vertically and horizontally, is slightly greater than that of the average notebook (the second generation is also thinner and lighter than the first). It does not have a keypad, but relies on a multi-touch screen interface as its method of basic input. However, it can be docked to an external, optional keyboard accessory for extended typing.

The iPad sports a multi-touch screen that is interacted with directly. You scroll a webpage by flicking it with your finger. You choose tools in an application by tapping them. You can “zoom out” of photos on the screen by pinching them with your fingers, or enlarge your view by “reverse pinching” or spreading your fingers apart. Front and rear cameras on the iPad 2 allow for both HD video capture and FaceTime video chat.

It’s important to note that the iPad is not intended to replace your Mac or PC, but rather to be a supplement to it. You will want to plug in your iPad to your desktop or laptop in order to sync data between the devices, to update the OS, and possibly to recharge the iPad (although recharging docks are also available).

The iPad can be used for some serious work using the iWork software suite, Apple’s answer to Microsoft Office. It allows for the creation of documents, spreadsheets and slide show presentations.

The biggest appeal and draw of the iPad, however, is as an information appliance. It is ideal for the consumption of information and entertainment in the form of web browsing, reading, and gaming. The iPad is powered by thousands of apps that appeal to every conceivable interest.

There are certainly plenty of entertainment apps to choose from. Real racing HD allows you to take the wheel through seaside circuits, forests and burning deserts. The whole iPad becomes your steering wheel as you tilt, steering with the built in accelerometer. Marvel comics for the iPad allows you to keep up with your favorite superheroes on an interface it’s remarkably like reading a real, printed comic book, and Alphabet Fun helps children learn their ABCs.

The default browser on the iPad, not surprisingly, is Apple’s own Safari, which works in much the same way as Safari on the Mac or PC, except that it does not support Flash. Some users may miss this, while others applaud the speed boost that Apple’s omission provides. Lately, alternative browsers have also been appearing on the iPad, including Opera Mini.

The crisp screen resolution of the iPad does justice to photographs, videos and e-books. An adequate speaker system provides output for music and audio, and a headphone jack provides for enhanced listening pleasure.

Internet connectivity is provided by WiFi and 3G, which offers the highest speed in wireless Internet technology and overrides the speed of the Wi-Fi hotspot available in any particular location. To connect to a 3G network, you must insert a 3G SIM card and then you can switch from a WI-FI to 3G connection anytime.

With its 9.7 inch LED backlit multi-touch display, its web and e-mail integration and its thousands of available apps, the iPad may well become your favorite information and entertainment appliance.
