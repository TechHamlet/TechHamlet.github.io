The One Thing Holding Back the Tablet Market

Jan 15th, 2012 | Comments

When it comes to portable computing devices, nothing tops the tablet PC. With a killer combination of speed and size, tablets can let users do more wherever they are. More and more consumers are realizing this, as millions of them have added tablets to their gadget repertoire. 2012 could be an even better year, as Apple’s iPad 3, along with high-powered Android tablets running the Ice Cream Sandwich operating system, could push tablets further into the mainstream. There is just one thing holding back tablets from becoming primary computing devices.

Why tablets can replace laptops

It might not seem true, but tablet PCs can certainly act as a laptop replacement. In fact, laptops are moving in a distinctly tablet direction. The latest trend is something called the Ultrabook, which is essentially a super thin laptop that doesn’t have an optical drive. Apple started this trend a few years ago with the MacBook Air, and now PCs are following in the same direction. The idea is to create a light, portable computing device that retains most of a laptop’s power.

While Ultrabooks certainly accomplish that end, they’re still considerably larger than tablets. They require users to flip open the device to use it, which means a mechanical hinge. It also means propping up the device on something, whether it be a lap, coffee table, or desk. Worse, few, if any, have touchscreen interfaces. There’s nothing wrong with Ultrabooks, really, except that they are the last realistic iteration of the laptop. From here laptops can no longer evolve. They can only change completely.

Tablets represent a logical transformation. They’re one solid slate, and users can hold them up if need be. The biggest screens, at around 10 inches, are still smaller than a typical small laptop, but the ability to hold it closer to the face can sometimes make up for that. Even 7-inch tablets have on-screen keyboard that are just as usable as physical ones, and there’s always the option to use a Bluetooth keyboard. They’re obviously lighter, and are much easier to store and carry around in a bag.

While the sheer processor power of tablets isn’t quite at the level of laptops, it’s only a matter of time before we get to that point. Again, manufacturers are fitting Ultrabooks with powerful chips, and in due time those same chips will fit into a tablet’s shell. There’s also the issue of battery life. Recent energy efficiency trends have greatly benefitted tablets, giving them battery lives of 10, 12, or even 15 hours. Laptops might get there in time, but by that time tablets will catch up in terms of processing power. At that point we’re back at the physical differences, which favor the tablet.

So why, then, will it take a while for people to use tablets as they do laptops? The answer boils down to one issue.

The aura of unfamiliarity

Simply put, people aren’t yet familiar with tablets. By this point, the desktop computer is plenty familiar. That makes the laptop familiar, since it is basically a shrunken down version of the desktop. Laptops run the same operating systems, have the same physical keyboard layout, handle CDs – basically, anything you can do on a desktop you can do on a laptop (provided it has enough processing power and RAM).

Tablets, on the other hand, are something completely new. As mentioned above, they’re a complete step forward, rather than an evolution. They don’t have physical keyboard at all, meaning that people have to learn to type without any tactile feedback. They have touchscreen interfaces, which is something completely removed from the laptop and desktop experiences. The operating systems are also different, which chances the equation greatly. Android is different than anything you’d see running on a mainstream laptop.

People see these differences in platforms and think that they must be complementary devices. Tablets might be nice, and people can probably think of dozens of ways to use them in everyday life. But at this point, they’re different enough from desktops and laptops that they’re not considered viable replacements. At the same time, anyone who uses a tablet extensively knows that it can replace a laptop in almost every way.

We could see some of those walls torn down in 2012. Increased processing power will go a long way in making the tablet a laptop replacement. Merely being a year older will help as well, since familiarity is a big issue here. As people get to know tablets more intimately, they will learn that they can, indeed be replacements for their laptops.

Remaining issues

Still, there are some remaining issues with the transition from laptops to tablets. Some of them are bigger than others, but none is too large to prevent people from making the switch.

  • Flash. While Android does run Flash, it doesn’t run it particularly well. The iPad doesn’t run it at all. It does appear, however, that Flash is on its way out. Once more services make the switch to HTML5, the Flash on tablets issue should fall by the wayside.

  • Closed architecture. This is more of an issue with the iPad than it is Android tablets, but it still exists as an issue for Android tabs. Tablets simply aren’t as open as PCs. They’re close, thanks to Android, but not quite there.

  • Storage. Laptops and desktops have traditional hard drives, which means they can pack in the storage. Seeing a 2-terabyte hard drive in a desktop is nothing noteworthy these days. Tablets, on the other hand, seem to max out at 64GB. This might be enough for a lightly used device, but not a primary one. But as solid state drives grow in capacity, this issue will become less of a hindrance. Cloud storage could help as well.

Plenty of people have already stopped using their laptops in favor of desktops. (I have to keep my laptop plugged in all the time, or else it will have no battery remaining when I want to use it. That’s how infrequently I use it.) Even more will make the switch in 2012. The further development of the tablet as a primary device will continue, and as more people become familiar with the device the more people will adopt it. We’re only a few years away from a tablet-dominated society.
